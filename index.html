About

Who I Am

I am a Doctor in Astronomy and Licenciado en Ciencias Físicas, working at the Argentine Institute of Radio Astronomy (IAR) within the Pulsar Monitoring in Argentina (PuMA) collaboration. My research focuses on the intensive radio monitoring of southern hemisphere pulsars, with particular emphasis on detecting and characterizing glitches — sudden spin-up events that offer a unique window into the extreme physics of neutron star interiors.

I obtained my Licenciatura in Physical Sciences from the University of Buenos Aires (GPA 9.57/10) and completed my PhD in Astronomy at IAR / FCAG-UNLP in 2026. My doctoral thesis, "Intensive Monitoring of Southern Hemisphere Pulsars," was supervised by Dr. Federico García (IAR) and co-supervised by Dr. Santiago del Palacio (Chalmers University of Technology, Sweden).

Beyond research, I teach physics at UBA. I also contribute to science outreach and serve as a referee for The Astrophysical Journal.

Research

What I Study

Pulsar Glitches

Pulsars are extraordinarily stable rotators, yet some experience sudden spin-up events called glitches. These events are thought to arise from interactions between the superfluid interior of neutron stars and their solid crusts. I detect, characterize, and model these glitches using high-cadence radio observations from the IAR's two 30-meter antennas operating at 1400 MHz. My work has led to the detection of glitches in the Vela pulsar, PSR J0742−2822, PSR J1048−5832, and PSR J1740−3015, among others.

Pulsar Timing

I use the pulsar timing technique to model the times of arrival (TOAs) of radio pulses and study rotational irregularities with high precision. This includes the analysis of timing noise, red noise, spin-down rate oscillations, and transient exponential recoveries following glitches. I work with standard timing tools including Tempo2 and PRESTO.

Gravitational Waves & Multi-messenger Astronomy

I contribute pulsar ephemerides to the LIGO–Virgo–KAGRA (LVK) collaboration for both continuous gravitational-wave searches and targeted searches triggered by pulsar glitches. The high-cadence ephemerides built from IAR data have been instrumental in constraining gravitational-wave emission models — including the search associated with the 2024 Vela glitch — connecting radio astronomy to fundamental physics.
